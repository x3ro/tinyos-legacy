Project Blaze

CC1100 and CC2500 Dual Radio Stack Development

 

 

There are many compelling reasons for moving to the
CC1100 / CC1101 / CC2500 radios.

We are spearheading this effort by creating a software radio stack capable of supporting both single- and dual-radio platforms. This software is dubbed the "Blaze" radio stack.

If you would like to help with this development or have a platform that features CC1100 or CC2500 radios, please contact David Moss at dmm at rincon dot com.

 

 

These new TI/Chipcon radios offer significant advantages over the archaic designs of the CC1000 and CC2420. Below is a comparison.

1. Software and Pin Compatibility

The CC1100 and CC2500 radios are completely software driver and pin compatible. We can reach 315 MHz, 433 MHz, 900 MHz, and 2.4 GHz by only replacing the chip and its matching network. Everything else, including software, stays the same.

We can run two radios using one software stack.

At 315 MHz, we have reached ranges of 750+ feet on grass at +10 dBm and wire antennas.

 

2. Power Consumption

The CC1100 and CC2500 support a hardware-driven low power listening implementation, or Wake-on-Radio (WoR). Your microcontroller can remain in the deepest sleep possible while the radio takes care of its own duty cycling.

 

3. Throughput

The CC1100 and CC2500 are capable of communicating at 500 kbps maximum.

Compare that to the CC2420 which reaches a maximum of 250 kbps, or the CC1000 which exhibits a lazy 76.8 kbps.

The faster you communicate, the less time your radio has to be consuming power to get messages through your network.

 

4. Modulation Techniques

The CC2420 only supports DSSS. The CC1000 only supports FSK and OOK.

The CC1100 and CC2500 radios support FSK, OOK, MSK, and GFSK, giving you more options to choose from.

Plus, with everyone using DSSS in the 2.4 GHz spectrum, the CC2500 has room to grow. DSSS is no longer a limitation.

 

5. Packaging

The CC1100 and CC2500 come in a 4 mm x 4 mm package.

Something that size is ridiculously small in comparison to the CC2420 and CC1000 radios.

 

Development

Development so far has taken place on hacked up TMotes. We have obtained some CC1100EM and CC2500EM evaluation modules and wired them by hand to our nodes, calling it a "tmote1100" and "tmote2500". This gives us a stable platform on which to build and test our software radio drivers.

Development has proceeded through a continuous integration process, where unit tests are written using TUnit to verify code integrity running on actual hardware. This has proven to be an invaluable tool to the development process, and the quality and robustness of our code reflects it.
